Volume over zeta

The chapter examines how the value $\zeta(2) = \dfrac{\pi^2}{6}$ behaves like a spatial ratio that links surface area and volume through scale-invariant steps. We use simple ratios to keep spatial interpretations honest, and delay any operation that would smuggle in geometry where the algorithm itself provides none.

The structure of volume over zeta

Write $\zeta(2)=\dfrac{\pi^2}{6}$. Treat this as a ratio that “sits in the middle” of a three-stage cycle: an initial stage $V_{\text{init}}$, a middle stage $V_{\text{sa}}$ (tied to a surface area), and a next stage $V_{\text{next}}$. The working relations (labels (02.02)–(02.05) in the OSF text) can be summarized:

\[ \zeta(2)=\frac{\pi^2}{6}, \qquad V_{\text{init}} \;\approx\; \tfrac{1}{8}\,V_{\text{sa}}, \qquad V_{\text{next}} \;\approx\; \tfrac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}}\,V_{\text{sa}} . \]

Interpreted via scale-free ratios, this reads as a snapshot of a sphere inflating by doubling radius, with the surface-area factor and volume factor staying constant across steps.

The scale-invariant ratios

The cycle $V_{\text{init}} \to V_{\text{sa}} \to V_{\text{next}}$ uses constant quotients: doubling radius multiplies surface area by $4$ and volume by $8$. Those constants let us talk about geometry without importing units.

Geometric progression view

Recall $\zeta(2)=\sum_{n\ge 1} n^{-2}=\prod_{p}(1-p^{-2})^{-1}$. The additive and multiplicative views mirror geometric sequences: doubling radii, $4\times$ area, $8\times$ volume—each a fixed ratio.

Discrete vs continuous

The $\zeta(2)$ algorithm is rational and discrete. The scale-invariant ratios let us read off spherical features without invoking physical space; we avoid operations that assert lengths or volumes beyond what the ratios themselves encode.

Ab initio principle

The provisional “ab initio” principle (developed in later chapters) is intended to bridge this abstract ratio to observed spatial phenomena, while staying faithful to the arithmetic constraints.

Mappings: circumference and surface area

For small $s$, proportional mappings respect the pattern: the numerator’s derivative suggests a $2\pi$-scaled circumference, while higher-order structure maps to $\pi^2$-scaled surface area, with denominators adjusted proportionally to keep the value equal to $\zeta(s)$.

A toy summary (schematic, not a proof):

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{circumference} &\propto 2\pi \cdot r, \\ \text{surface area} &\propto \pi^2 \cdot r^2, \\ \text{volume (ratio)}&\propto \zeta(2) = \frac{\pi^2}{6}. \end{aligned} \]
