Volume over zeta

The chapter examines how the value $\\zeta(2)=\\pi^2/6$ behaves like a spatial ratio that links surface area and volume through scale-invariant steps. We use simple ratios to keep spatial interpretations honest, and delay any operation that would smuggle in geometry where the algorithm itself provides none.

The structure of Volume over zeta

Write $\\zeta(2)=\\dfrac{\\pi^2}{6}$. Treat the value as a ratio that “sits in the middle” of a three-stage cycle: an initial volume $V_{\\text{init}}$, a middle stage $V_{\\text{sa}}$ (tied to a surface area), and a next volume $V_{\\text{next}}$. The working relations (labels (02.02)–(02.05) in the OSF text) are summarized:

$\\displaystyle \\zeta(2)=\\frac{\\pi^2}{6},\\qquad V_{\\text{init}}\\;\\approx\\;\\frac{1}{8}V_{\\text{sa}},\\qquad V_{\\text{next}}\\;\\approx\\;\\frac{\\pi}{2\\sqrt{2}}\\,V_{\\text{sa}}.$

Interpreted via scale-free ratios, this reads as a snapshot of a sphere inflating by doubling radius.

The scale invariant ratios

The cycle $V_{\\text{init}} \\to V_{\\text{sa}} \\to V_{\\text{next}}$ uses constant quotients (volume $\\,\\times 8$ for each radius doubling) and pins a surface-area factor of $4$ as the common ratio between consecutive stages when viewed appropriately. Those constants are what let us talk about geometry without importing units.

Geometric progression

Recall $\\zeta(2)=\\sum_{n\\ge 1} n^{-2} = \\prod_{p}(1-p^{-2})^{-1}$. The additive and multiplicative views mirror geometric sequences: doubling radii, area factors of $4$, volume factors of $8$.

Progression and common ratios

Numerator-like terms scale by $2$ (radius), denominator-like terms by $8$ (volume). The overall ratio keeps a constant surface-area factor of $4$ between consecutive stages.

Inevitable self organisation

The inverse-square footprint and the geometric progressions conspire to yield a stable “surface-first” description. The infinite sum/product locks the common ratio at the completion stage of the cycle.

Making sense of zeta

The cleanest reading is to treat surface area as primary: the numerator/denominator structure exposes area growth, and the resulting “volume” is what wrapped area traces.

Contrasting discrete and continuous aspects

The $\\zeta(2)$ algorithm is purely rational and discrete. The scale-invariant ratios let us read off spherical features without invoking physical space.

Detached from spatial dimensions

No spatial dataset is being sampled; we are reading geometry out of number-theoretic structure alone. That’s why we avoid operations that silently assert “volume” or “radius” unless the ratio demands it.

Ab initio principle

The conjectured pre-mathematical principle (developed later) is intended to bridge this abstract ratio to observed spatial phenomena.

From abstract spatial to observed continuity

We interpret the value as a spatial ratio because it carries the same invariants a true spherical process would, but we keep everything dimensionless.

Consider the following formulas

When a mapping requires a radius or area, we derive the powered quantity but stop short of taking roots that would presume a spatial context not granted by the series.

Not from a spatial context or abstraction thereof

Example: taking $\\sqrt[3]{V}$ presupposes “volume”. In this notebook we keep $r^3$ as the working quantity when a step speaks about “radius” embedded in a cubic relation, rather than reducing it to $r$ as if a physical $V$ had been measured.

Spherical scale-invariant proportion

Assume $\\zeta(s)$ can be mapped proportionally to spherical measures by differentiating the numerator and keeping the denominator proportional, yielding clean $2\\pi$ and $\\pi^2$ targets.

Mapping to a circumference
